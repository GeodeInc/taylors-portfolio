Should AI be allowed in schools?

An exploration of AI as a taught literacy skill — and why the answer isn't as simple as yes or no.

AuthorTaylor Houghtaling
CourseCollege Writing
TermSpring 2026
Thesis

Students are already using AI. The question isn't whether to allow it — it's whether their education will be part of that conversation, or whether schools will leave them to figure it out on their own.

01
The workplace already uses AI. Schools should catch up.

In 2023, McKinsey found that one-third of organizations were already regularly using generative AI in at least one business function, with 40 percent planning to increase that investment. Two years later, Wharton's research showed 92 percent planning to increase it — more than double. By early 2026, Gallup's national survey of nearly 24,000 workers found 41 percent reporting org-wide AI integration, with 65 percent of those workers saying AI improved their productivity. Morgan Stanley confirmed the output: an average 11.5 percent net productivity gain across sectors most exposed to AI.

That trajectory matters for students. A first-year enrolling today graduates in 2029 into a labor market shaped by those numbers. The window to give them real AI skills — not just "here's ChatGPT" but genuinely critical, practiced fluency — is narrowing. If schools wait until the conversation feels more settled, they will spend four years producing graduates who are behind on day one.

The argument that AI "does the work for students" misunderstands what real AI use looks like. At Tenzor LLC, a software startup I co-founded, I use AI regularly while building client software — things like a checkout flow for a gym's point-of-sale system, or a member PIN verification component. I ask AI to surface approaches, then I evaluate them, rewrite them to fit the codebase, and make the actual decisions. That is not the AI doing the work. That is a tool in a skilled person's hands. Teaching students to operate that way is what AI literacy means.

The students who graduate knowing how to do that — evaluate, integrate, decide — are the ones employers are going to want. The ones who never learned because their school banned the tool will figure it out eventually. It would just be better if their education had been part of it.

"The choice isn't between a world where students use AI and one where they don't. It's between students who use it thoughtlessly and students whose education prepared them to use it well."
Central Argument

A common fear is that using AI to help write essays will make students worse writers. Research suggests the opposite when AI is used as a process tool rather than a product generator. In their 2023 SSRN paper, Ethan Mollick and Lilach Mollick of the Wharton School propose frameworks for AI as a tutor, coach, and feedback tool — all roles that scaffold student thinking rather than replace it.

Students who use AI as a drafting scaffold — generating outlines, getting feedback on organization, or exploring multiple phrasings — can direct more cognitive energy toward argumentation and clarity, consistent with decades of research on scaffolded writing instruction. Think of it this way: a student who struggles with blank-page anxiety can use AI to generate a rough first paragraph, then immediately recognize what's wrong with it, revise it, and understand why their version is better. That is active learning.

There is also a significant equity argument. Not every student has a parent who is a skilled writer, access to private tutors, or the confidence to visit office hours. AI writing tools democratize access to feedback that was previously only available to students with those resources. A first-generation college student who has never been taught how to structure a thesis argument can use AI as a patient, available, always-on guide — not to cheat, but to learn.

02
AI builds writing skills — it doesn't replace them.
03
Banning AI doesn't stop it. Teaching it shapes it.

Some schools have responded to the rise of AI by banning its use and deploying AI-detection software. This approach has serious problems. Research covered in Education Week found that AI detection tools carry high false-positive rates — a Stanford University study found that over half of TOEFL essays written by non-native speakers were incorrectly flagged as AI-generated, while native-speaker essays passed (Najarro, 2023). A false accusation of cheating can have lasting consequences on a student's academic record and their relationship with school.

As New York Times technology columnist Kevin Roose argued in January 2023, banning ChatGPT simply will not work: students have phones, personal laptops, and VPNs — access to AI tools regardless of school network policy. The more effective approach is AI-integrated pedagogy: explicitly teaching students when, how, and why to use AI tools, and designing assignments that require demonstrated human thought even when AI assistance is permitted.

Teaching AI literacy also opens up conversations about its limitations — hallucinated facts, biased outputs, stylistic flatness — which are critical thinking exercises in their own right. A student who learns to critically evaluate an AI-generated paragraph is practicing exactly the kind of source skepticism that English and research classes have always aimed to build.

Conclusion

The question is already being answered — by students.

I am not arguing that AI is harmless or that every student will use it responsibly. Some won't. But the response to that cannot be to pretend the technology doesn't exist, or to deploy detection tools that the research shows do not work reliably and punish the wrong students when they fail.

From 2023 to 2026, the share of companies planning to increase AI investment went from 40 percent to 92 percent. That is the world students are heading into. Schools that teach AI literacy — honestly, critically, with real attention to where it fails — are preparing students for that world. Schools that don't are leaving students to figure it out alone.

They will figure it out. It would just be better if their education had been part of it.
